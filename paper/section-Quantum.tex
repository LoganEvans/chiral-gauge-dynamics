\section{Quantum Mechanics Without Quantization}
\label{sec:Quantum}

In the previous section, we established that particles exist; in this section, we will explore how they behave. As we discussed in Section \ref{sec:Introduction}, \spinFourC naturally possesses a bispinor structure, so we will evaluate the Dirac operator $\slashed{D}$ by treating the local gauge connection as the spinor field $(\Psi = \mathcal{A}_\nu)$. This may appear at first to be a mismatch between a vector-valued 1-form (connection) and a bispinor, but the $4 \times 4$ matrix representation of $\spinFourCAlg \cong \slTwoCLAlg \oplus \slTwoCRAlg$ Lie algebra indices for the 1-form possess the same algebraic degrees of freedom as a Dirac spinor. By considering the gauge-covariant derivative and the Yang-Mills curvature tensor, we find
\begin{equation}
    D_\mu \mathcal{A}_\nu = F_{\mu\nu} + \partial_\nu \mathcal{A}_\mu .
    \quad \leanref{CGD.Quantum.covariantSpinorDeriv_eq_curvature_plus_deriv}
    \label{eq:covariantSpinorIdentity}
\end{equation}
\noindent When we contract this identity with the tetrad field $e_a^\mu$ and the Dirac gamma matrices $\gamma^a$, we obtain
\begin{equation}
    \slashed{D}_\nu \Psi = e_a^\mu \gamma^a F_{\mu\nu} + e_a^\mu \gamma^a (\partial_\nu \mathcal{A}_\mu)
    \quad \leanref{CGD.Quantum.generalizedDynamicDiracEquation}
    \label{eq:generalizedDynamicDiracEquation}
\end{equation}
\noindent This is the generalized Dirac Equation. In the first term, $F_{\mu\nu}$ is the interaction between a fermion and a gauge field. In the second term, $\partial_\nu \mathcal{A}_\mu$ is an inertial term that absorbs the inhomogeneous shift $(\mathcal{A}_\nu \rightarrow U \mathcal{A}_\nu U^{-1} + U \partial_\nu U^{-1})$. This means that particles feel a force from the background if the background universe is dynamically bubbling or expanding, but if the universe is stationary, the second term goes to zero and we recover the standard definition for the Dirac Equation. Notice that the spacetime index $\nu$ survives the Dirac contraction because the spinor field is the gauge connection itself.

Since CGD is a classical field theory for both gravity and particles, one of the biggest concerns is how it addresses Bell's Theorem. \citeauthor{bell1964einstein} concluded:
\begin{quote}
    In a theory in which parameters are added to quantum mechanics to determine the results of individual measurements, without changing the statistical predictions, there must be a mechanism whereby the set- ting of one measuring device can influence the reading of another instrument, however remote. Moreover, the signal involved must propagate instantaneously, so that such a theory could not be Lorentz invariant. \citep{bell1964einstein}
\end{quote}
\noindent This is colloquially summarized as ``No local, hidden-variables theory explains Quantum Mechanics.'' This is a problem for a theory like CGD because classical theories must be local hidden-variable theories in order to satisfy Lorentz Invariance.

We established in Equation \ref{eq:topologicalActionVariationZero} that CGD has no classical equations of motion, so the local gauge field is underdetermined. In order to explore one of the possible solutions to the local geometry, we propose the following ansatz:
\begin{align}
    \mathcal{A}^{\text{tube}}_\mu(x) &= \begin{cases}
      0 & \mu = 0 \\
      i \sigma_3 & \mu = 1 \\
      i \sigma_1 & \mu = 2 \\
      i \sigma_2 & \mu = 3
   \end{cases} \quad \leanref{CGD.Quantum.fluxTubeFrame} \label{eq:fluxTubeFrame} \\
   \exists \gamma(t), \quad \gamma(0) &= x, \quad \gamma(1) = y \quad \leanref{CGD.Quantum.areEntangled} \label{eq:areEntangled}
\end{align}
\noindent This ``flux tube frame'' defines entanglement for points $x$ and $y$ by connecting them with a curve where gauge rotations are allowed at every point of the curve $\gamma(t)$. We verify this is a valid physical solution in \texttt{CGD.Quantum.fluxTubeIsMinimal}\leanref{CGD.Quantum.fluxTubeIsMinimal} by proving that it satisfies the minimal-energy boundary condition for a static magnetic field. Since this construct is 1-dimensional, Equation \ref{eq:kinematicSingleColorDegeneracy} shows that the determinanant collapses and the metric vanishes within the tube, giving:
\begin{equation}
    \det(g_{\mu\nu}) = 0 \quad \text{along the ER=EPR bridge}
    \quad \leanref{CGD.Quantum.kinematicEntanglementWormhole}
    \label{eq:kinematicEntanglementWormhole}
\end{equation}
\noindent This acts as a topological bridge.

Now, we can evaluate the path integral along the flux tube, and find that we recover the trigonometric coefficients of quantum state vectors
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{P}\exp\left(\int \mathcal{A}_\mu dx^\mu\right) = \cos(\theta) \cdot I + i \sin(\theta) \cdot M \quad \leanref{CGD.Quantum.fluxTubeHolonomyEvaluation}
\end{equation}
\noindent We also find that since the core of the flux tube is commutative, the phase is conserved and matches the CHSH Tsirelson bound:
\begin{equation}
    \left[ \text{Re}(\text{CHSH}) \right]^2 \leq 8 .
    \quad \leanref{CGD.Quantum.geometricHolonomyTsirelsonBound}
    \label{eq:geometricHolonomyTsirelsonBound}
\end{equation}

The result in Equation \ref{eq:geometricHolonomyTsirelsonBound} demonstrates that contrary to conventional belief, a purely classical theory can satisfy Bell's inequalities. We did not invalidate any part of Bell's theorem. Since $\det(g) = 0$, the metric inside the flux tube vanishes and the geometric distance between the particles is zero, so the interaction is strictly local in the topological sense even though it appears to be non-local to an observer.

While this resolves the most obvious concern about proposing CGD as an ontology for quantum mechanics, entanglement correlation is not the only concern. Since we proved in Equation \ref{eq:topologicalActionVariationZero} that CGD does not possess a classical Hamiltonian, the Dirac operator in Equation \ref{eq:generalizedDynamicDiracEquation} is not sufficient by itself to explain the complex phase rotation of quantum time-evolution. For this, we need to address ``the problem of time'' \citep{rovelli1996relational}.

We will again use the flux tube ansatz and note that the path-integral of a connection $\mathcal{A}$ over distance $t$ is the matrix exponential of the field multiplied by the distance $(e^{t \mathcal{A}})$. The spatial holonomy along the \uOne core of the flux tube mathematically mirrors $e^{-iHt}$, meaning time evolution is just relational geometric phase:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{P} \exp \left( \int \mathcal{A}_1 dx^1 \right) = \exp(-i H t) .
    \quad \leanref{CGD.Quantum.Holonomy.relationalTimeEmergence}
    \label{eq:relationalTimeEmergence}
\end{equation}

The next concern we will address is how we obtain a ``quantum'' from a classical theory. Canonical quantization is a mathematical process that turns a classical theory into a quantum theory, and by treating classical quantities such as position or momentum as operators with the axiomatic non-commutation property $[\hat{q}, \hat{p}] = i \hbar$, physicists can obtain probability waves and discrete energy levels. In CGD, we do not have the non-commutation property, so if quanta exist, they must emerge from the topology. However, since the background of the \spinFourC is a macroscopic \suTwo field, the \suTwo group manifold is a 3-dimensional sphere $S^3$. If we take a topological defect $(F^{(R)}_{\mu\nu} \neq 0)$, we can enclose it in another $S^3$ sphere at spatial infinity. At this boundary, we have a mathematical mapping $S^3 \to S^3$. To obtain quantization, we use the Cartan-Maurer Degree Theorem \citep{nakahara2003geometry}, which shows that it is only possible to wrap a sphere with another sphere an integer number of times without tearing the manifold ($\pi_3(\suTwo) = \mathbb{Z}$).

Next, we consider the Pontryagin density that we proved is the unique CGD Lagrangian in Equation \ref{eq:topologicalLagrangianUniqueness}. Stokes' theorem proves that the volume integral of a derivative is equal to the value at its boundary, so when we integrate the 4-dimensional action, we find that that action possessed by the particle is quantized and the Cartan-Maurer integral evaluates to \citep{belavin1975pseudoparticle, nakahara2003geometry}:
\begin{equation}
    S_{\text{top}} \in \{ -1, 1 \} .
    \quad \leanref{CGD.Quantum.kinematicActionQuantization}
    \label{eq:kinematicActionQuantization}
\end{equation}
\noindent In CGD, the ``quantum'' is the geometric winding number for a topological defect.

Finally, we will address the measurement problem by using statistical mechanics. In Section \ref{sec:Particles} we proved that particles are \suTwo defects. The geometry of every point in \suTwo is a 3-sphere $(S^3)$ which we can decompose into a 2-sphere $(S^2)$ where every point on the sphere is attached to a 1-dimensional circle (a \uOne fiber). This decomposition matches laboratory equipment, because a detector such as a magnet exists in 3-dimensional space, so it can only measure values on a $S^2$ sphere. The actual \uOne phase of a particle is a value on the \uOne fiber. When we use the Liouville volume-preservation theorem \citep{arnold1989mathematical} to calculate the phase-space volume of the Hopf Fibration, we find
\begin{equation}
    P = \frac{\int_{\theta}^{\pi} d\mathcal{V}_{\text{Hopf}}}{\int_{0}^{\pi} d\mathcal{V}_{\text{Hopf}}} = 1 - \frac{M}{E_0} .
    \quad \leanref{CGD.Quantum.Measurement.kinematicBornRuleEquivalence}
    \label{eq:kinematicBornRuleEquivalence}
\end{equation}
\noindent This is exactly the Born Rule.
