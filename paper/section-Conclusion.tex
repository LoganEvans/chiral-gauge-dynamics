\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:Conclusion}

We have presented a theory that is more notable for what it does not do than for what it accomplishes. CGD does not assume an independent background metric, it does not assume extra dimensions, and perhaps most importantly, it does not assume fundamental quantization of particles and forces. Starting with \spinFourC and the pure-connection research pioneered by \citet{urbantke1984integrability}, \citet{ashtekar1986new}, \citet{capovilla1989general}, \citet{krasnov2011plebanski} (and many others!) we presented a theory, formalized in Lean4, that demonstrates that a classical theory is a viable fundamental ontology. The classical math already contains the mass gap, entanglement bounds, and gravity.

However, math doesn't dictate reality; data dictates reality. So far, this research has only made casual contact with hard data when we derived the necessity of an axial-vector condensate and performed a sanity check against a single proton radius puzzle dataset. While we make the claim that this classical theory is an alternative to quantized physics, only hard data will determine if it is any good at describing the universe we live in.
